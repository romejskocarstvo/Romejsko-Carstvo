What Was the Eastern Roman Empire?

The Eastern Roman Empire, most commonly referred to in modern historiography as the Byzantine Empire, was the direct continuation of the Roman Empire.

The term “Byzantine Empire — Byzantium” is a modern historiographical label and was completely unknown to its contemporaries. The empire’s own name was Ῥωμανία (Romania) or Βασιλεία Ῥωμαίων (Basileia Romaion), a literal translation of the Latin phrase: Imperium Romanorum (Empire of the Romans), and all surviving sources call its inhabitants Romans (Rhomaioi).

The name itself was first used in the 16th century (1557) in the work of the German humanist and historian Hieronymus Wolf, “Corpus Historiae Byzantinae”, to facilitate the classification of medieval Greek historical sources. It derives from the ancient Greek name Βυζάντιον (Byzantion), the name of a Greek colony on the Bosporus where Constantinople was later founded.

This name also had political consequences: by denying continuity with the Roman Empire, it made it easier for Western rulers and the Church to claim the Roman legacy, while the eastern state was portrayed as Greek and Orthodox, rather than as the sole legitimate continuation of the Roman Empire.

This empire survived for over 1,100 years — from the consecration of Constantinople in 330 to the fall of the city under Ottoman rule in 1453, making it the longest-lasting state entity in human history.

The capital of the empire was Constantinople, modern-day Istanbul, strategically located on the strait between Europe and Asia. Throughout its thousand-year history, the Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantium) was the center of Orthodox Christian civilization, the guardian of Greek culture, philosophy, and literature, and the keeper of the Roman legal system.

The role of the Eastern Roman Empire in preserving ancient heritage was crucial for the development of world civilization. Eastern Roman scholars preserved and copied the works of Plato, Aristotle, Homer, and other ancient authors, without which the European Renaissance and modern Western science would be unimaginable. The Empire developed rich Christian theology, philosophy, literature, and art, creating some of the most beautiful churches, icons, mosaics, and manuscripts in human history.

The cultural and civilizational influence of the Eastern Roman Empire far exceeded its political boundaries and left a lasting mark on all of European and world history. Eastern Roman missionaries Cyril, Methodius, and their disciples created the Cyrillic script and translated the Holy Bible into the Slavic language, thereby transmitting Christianity and literacy to the Slavic peoples. Roman law became the foundation of the legal systems of many European countries, while Eastern Roman architecture and art shaped the Byzantine style that spread from Russia to Italy.

Today, the Eastern Roman legacy is reflected in architecture, art, religion, literature, and state traditions across Europe and the world — from the Balkans to Italy and Western Europe. Without the Eastern Roman Empire, modern Europe would not be the same: its influence shaped Christian civilization, preserved ancient knowledge for future generations, and laid the foundations for the development of modern European culture, science, and statehood.

Why Is the Eastern Roman Empire Important?

The Eastern Roman Empire was the bridge between the ancient and the modern world. It preserved the knowledge of the ancient Greeks and Romans, developed Christian theology and art, and transmitted culture to the Slavic peoples.

Without the Eastern Roman Empire, today's Europe would not be the same. Its influence can be seen in the architecture, art, law, religion, and language of many peoples.
