330–518

From the founding of Constantinople to the accession of Justin I to the imperial throne.

The Beginning of a New Era

The period from 330 to 518 represents the time when the foundations of the state we now call the Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantium) were laid. Although its inhabitants still considered themselves Romans, during these centuries new political, religious, and cultural features were shaped that would mark the next thousand years of history.

When Emperor Constantine the Great solemnly consecrated Constantinople on May 11, 330, he did not found a new state. The Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantium) continued its uninterrupted existence, but the new capital gradually became the center of imperial power, military, trade, and the Christian faith.

The eastern provinces were wealthier, more numerous, and economically stronger than the western ones. Thanks to this, it was the East that would survive the crises that would lead to the collapse of the Western Roman Empire in the 5th century.

Constantine the Great and the New Rome

Constantine the Great (306–337) is one of the most important rulers in Eastern Roman history. His victory at the Battle of the Milvian Bridge in 312, the Edict of Milan in 313, and the founding of Constantinople permanently changed the course of European history.

The new capital was located on the Bosporus, at the crossroads between Europe and Asia. This position enabled Constantinople to become the wealthiest and best-fortified city of the Christian world during the coming centuries.

The Founding of Constantinople

On the site of the old Greek city of Byzantium, founded in the 7th century BC, Emperor Constantine the Great began the construction of the new capital of the Eastern Roman Empire. The city was solemnly consecrated on May 11, 330, and received the name Constantinople, while in official documents the name New Rome (Nova Roma) also appeared.

The choice of location was not accidental. Constantinople lay on the Bosporus, the strait connecting the Black Sea and the Mediterranean Sea. It was surrounded by the sea on three sides, and the land approach was easy to fortify.

The new capital was quickly adorned with forums, palaces, temples, public baths, aqueducts, cisterns, and the Hippodrome. Numerous artworks were brought to it from various parts of the empire.

Christianity and the New Capital

Constantine the Great remained remembered as the first Eastern Roman emperor who granted full freedom to Christianity. With the Edict of Milan in 313, the persecution of Christians ceased.

Constantinople gradually became the most important center of the Christian East. Over time, the Patriarch of Constantinople would become one of the most important figures in the Orthodox world.

In 325, Constantine convened the First Ecumenical Council in Nicaea. At the council, Arianism was condemned, and the largest part of the Nicene Creed was adopted.

After Constantine's Death

After the death of Constantine the Great in 337, power was taken over by his sons: Constantine II, Constantius II, and Constans. The division of power caused numerous conflicts within the imperial family, but the eastern part of the empire remained politically more stable than the western one.

Constantius II gradually became the sole ruler of the entire Eastern Roman Empire. During his reign, the construction of Constantinople continued, the development of state administration, and the strengthening of imperial power.

Although political struggles were frequent, it was precisely during this period that the process continued that would shape the eastern part of the empire into what we now call the Eastern Roman Empire during the coming centuries.

Julian — The Last Pagan Emperor

After the death of Constantius II in 361, his cousin Julian ascended the throne, one of the most educated rulers in Eastern Roman history. He was raised as a Christian but turned during his life to the old Greek-Roman religion, which is why he later received the nickname the Apostate.

Julian did not attempt to destroy Christianity by force but sought to restore traditional cults and return them to their former significance.

His reign was short. In 363, he led a great campaign against the Sasanian Empire. During one battle, Julian was mortally wounded and soon died.

His death ended the last serious attempt to restore paganism in the Eastern Roman Empire. Christianity continued its rise and became the permanent foundation of the state.

Jovian and Peace with Persia

After Julian's death, the army chose Jovian as emperor. His most important task was to save the army that found itself deep in enemy territory.

To secure the withdrawal, Jovian was forced to conclude an unfavorable peace with Persia. The Eastern Roman Empire ceded several border regions and fortresses, but the army was safely returned to the empire.

Jovian died already at the beginning of 364, after only a few months of reign.

Valentinian I and Valens

After Jovian's death, Valentinian I became emperor. Aware of the enormous size of the state, he appointed his brother Valens as co-ruler that same year.

While Valentinian I governed the western part of the empire, Valens ruled the East and faced the increasing pressure of the Goths on the Danube.

The Battle of Adrianople (Edirne) — 378

One of the heaviest defeats in the history of the Eastern Roman Empire occurred on August 9, 378, near Adrianople. Emperor Valens decided to attack the Goths without waiting for reinforcements.

In the battle, Emperor Valens himself was killed. The greater part of the eastern army was destroyed, which represented one of the greatest military defeats of the late Eastern Roman Empire.

Theodosius I the Great (379–395)

After the death of Valens, Theodosius I the Great became ruler of the eastern part of the empire. He restored the army, stabilized the Balkans, and strengthened imperial power.

With the Edict of Thessalonica in 380, Nicene Christianity became the official religion of the Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantium).

In 381, the Second Ecumenical Council in Constantinople was convened, at which the Nicene Creed was supplemented.

The Last Ruler of the United Roman Empire

After the victory over the usurper Eugenius in 394, Theodosius I united the entire Roman Empire under a single ruler for the last time.

After his death in 395, the eastern part of the empire with its capital in Constantinople was inherited by his son Arcadius.

The End of the Period 330–518

By 518, the Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantium) had undergone profound changes. Constantinople had become the most important political, military, and cultural center of the eastern Mediterranean.

All the foundations for the great age of Justin I and Justinian the Great had already been laid.
