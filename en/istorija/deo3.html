610–717

The age of Heraclius — great reforms, wars with Persia and the Arabs, and the beginning of the medieval character of the empire.

The Period of Great Changes

The reign of Heraclius (610–641) represents one of the decisive turning points in the history of the Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantium). His reforms, military successes and defeats, as well as the confrontation with new enemies — the Arabs — permanently changed the character of the state.

During this period, the empire lost large territories, but managed to survive and adapt to new conditions. The Greek language became dominant, and the state acquired increasingly medieval characteristics.

Heraclius — The Savior of the Empire

Heraclius came to power in 610, at a time when the empire was in deep crisis. The Persians had conquered Syria, Palestine, Egypt, and parts of Asia Minor, while the Slavs and Avars were ravaging the Balkans.

Heraclius launched a decisive counteroffensive. Between 622 and 628, he led a series of successful campaigns against Persia, and in 627, at the Battle of Nineveh, he inflicted a heavy defeat on the Persian king Khosrow II.

In 628, peace was concluded, by which the empire regained lost territories, including the True Cross, which was solemnly returned to Jerusalem.

The Rise of the Arabs and Loss of Provinces

Only a few years after Heraclius's successes, the Arabs appeared on the historical scene, united under the banner of Islam. As early as 634, they began great conquest campaigns.

The Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantium), exhausted by long wars with Persia, could not effectively resist. Soon Syria (636), Palestine (638), and Egypt (642) were lost. These territories represented the wealthiest and most populous provinces of the empire.

The loss of Egypt was a particularly heavy blow, as Egypt was the main supplier of grain for Constantinople.

Heraclius's Reforms

Aware of new challenges, Heraclius implemented a series of important reforms. The army was reorganized, and the system of themes was introduced — military-administrative districts that would become the basis of the empire's defense in the coming centuries.

The Greek language became the official language of the state and army, thus continuing a process that had begun in earlier centuries.

The Heraclian Dynasty (610–711)

After Heraclius's death in 641, his successors ascended the throne. The period was marked by internal conflicts, struggles for power, and further territorial losses.

The Arabs continued their conquests, and the empire was forced to pay tribute. Constantinople was successfully defended during the first Arab siege (674–678), thanks to the use of Greek fire.

The End of the Heraclian Dynasty

By 711, the Heraclian dynasty had lost control of the empire. A period of instability followed, with several emperors rapidly succeeding one another.

Only with the accession of Leo III the Isaurian in 717 did a new era begin — the age of the Isaurian dynasty and the Iconoclast controversies.
