1185–1261

From the fall of the Komnenoi to the restoration of the Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantium) and the return of Constantinople under the rule of the Palaiologoi.

The Angelos Dynasty

After the fall of Andronikos I Komnenos in 1185, the Angelos dynasty came to the throne. Their rule was marked by weakening of central authority, financial problems, and loss of influence that the Komnenoi had managed to restore.

The emperors of this dynasty failed to maintain the strength of the state. The army weakened, provinces became increasingly independent, and relations with Western powers became ever more complex.

The Road to the Fourth Crusade

At the beginning of the 13th century, relations between the Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantium) and the Western Crusaders were very tense. Instead of marching toward Jerusalem, the Crusader army in 1204 was directed toward Constantinople.

Internal struggles in the empire, a dispute over the throne, and the interests of Venice led to the Fourth Crusade changing its target and attacking the very capital of the Eastern Romans.

The Fall of Constantinople in 1204

In April 1204, the Crusaders conquered Constantinople. The city, which for centuries had been the center of Roman statehood and Christian culture, suffered great destruction and plunder.

The Crusaders established the Latin Empire, while the successors of the Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantium) created new states in exile, among which the most significant were the Empire of Nicaea, the Despotate of Epirus, and the Empire of Trebizond.

The Empire of Nicaea

After the fall of Constantinople, the most significant successor of the Eastern Roman Empire became the Empire of Nicaea. It managed to preserve the imperial tradition, state institutions, and the hope of returning to the capital.

The rulers of Nicaea, especially the Laskaris dynasty, gradually strengthened the state and expanded their rule in Asia Minor. The army and diplomacy enabled them to survive the most difficult period in the history of the empire.

Michael VIII Palaiologos

In 1259, Michael VIII Palaiologos took power in Nicaea. He managed to exploit the weakening of the Latin Empire and prepare the return of the Eastern Romans to their old capital.

On July 25, 1261, the army of General Alexios Strategopoulos entered Constantinople without major fighting. The Latin Empire ceased to exist, and the Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantium) was restored.

Restoration of the Empire

The return of Constantinople was a great symbolic success, but the state was no longer the great power from the time of the Komnenoi or the Macedonian dynasty. Territories were reduced, the economy weakened, and new enemies appeared in the east.

Nevertheless, the restoration in 1261 marked the beginning of the final period of the Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantium) — the rule of the Palaiologos dynasty.
