History of the Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantium)

More than a thousand years of history of the state that inherited the Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantium) and preserved Roman tradition until 1453.

Beginning of the Empire Chronology

From Rome to New Rome

The Eastern Roman Empire, known in modern historiography as Byzantium, was the continuation of the Roman Empire. Its inhabitants never called themselves Byzantines, but Romans.

After the division of the Roman Empire and the founding of Constantinople, the eastern part of the empire gradually developed a distinct political, cultural, and religious identity, but retained the Roman heritage.

The Founding of Constantinople

In 330, Emperor Constantine the Great proclaimed the New Rome, which would become the capital of the Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantium) for more than a thousand years.

Roman Heritage

The Eastern Romans preserved Roman law, imperial power, and the idea of a unified Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantium).

Christian Empire

Christianity became the foundation of state and cultural life, shaping art and society.

Great Periods of the History of the Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantium)

330

The Founding of Constantinople

Constantine the Great founds the New Rome on the Bosporus. The city becomes the new capital of the Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantium) and the center of the eastern world.

527–565

The Age of Justinian I

Justinian attempts to restore the old Eastern Roman Empire, introduces great legal reforms, and builds Hagia Sophia.

7th–9th Century

Crisis and Recovery

The Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantium) faces the Arabs, Slavs, and internal crises, but manages to survive and restore its strength.

867–1081

Macedonian Dynasty

The Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantium) experiences military and cultural prosperity. Basil II becomes one of the most powerful emperors.

1081–1185

Komnenian Restoration

Alexios I and Manuel I Komnenos restore the Eastern Roman Empire's (Byzantium's) strength through military reforms and diplomacy.

1261–1453

Palaiologan Era and Fall

The last dynasty attempts to preserve the Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantium), but Constantinople falls to the Ottomans in 1453.

Key Features of the Eastern Roman State (Byzantium)

Roman Law

The laws and legal system of the Eastern Romans influenced the development of European law.

Imperial Power

The Emperor was the supreme ruler of the Eastern Roman state (Byzantium), army, and protector of the Christian faith.

Military Power

From professional troops to the famous Greek fire, the army defended the Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantium) for centuries.

Culture and Science

Constantinople was the center of education, literature, art, and the preservation of ancient heritage.

The Legacy of the Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantium)

The Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantium) was one of the longest-lasting states in human history. For more than a thousand years, it preserved Roman state tradition, developed Christian culture, and created significant works in law, art, and architecture.

Its fall in 1453 did not mark the end of the Eastern Roman legacy. Roman law, Byzantine art, theology, and literature continued to influence European and world history.
