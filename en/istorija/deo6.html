1056–1185

From the weakening after the Macedonian dynasty to the Komnenian restoration and new crises of the empire.

Crisis After the Macedonian Dynasty

After the death of Empress Theodora in 1056, the rule of the Macedonian dynasty ended. The following decades were marked by rapid succession of emperors, weakening of central authority, and the growing influence of the military aristocracy.

At the same time, the empire faced new dangers. The Normans attacked the Italian possessions of the Eastern Romans, while in the east new enemies appeared — the Seljuk Turks.

The Battle of Manzikert

The heaviest blow to the empire was dealt in 1071 at the Battle of Manzikert. The Seljuk Sultan Alp Arslan defeated the army of Emperor Romanos IV Diogenes, who was captured.

After this defeat, the Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantium) lost a large part of Asia Minor, which for centuries had been the main military and economic base of the state.

Although the defeat did not immediately destroy the empire, it marked the beginning of a long-term crisis that would require major reforms and a new dynasty.

The Komnenian Dynasty

In 1081, Alexios I Komnenos ascended the throne, one of the most capable rulers of medieval Romaea. He inherited a weakened state and began a deep restoration of the army, finances, and administration.

Alexios I created a new system of alliances and diplomacy, using relations with Western Christian rulers to strengthen the empire's position.

The First Crusade

At the end of the 11th century, Alexios I Komnenos requested help from the West against the Seljuks. His call led to the launching of the First Crusade in 1096.

The Crusaders managed to conquer Jerusalem in 1099, but relations between the Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantium) and the Western allies soon became complex. Nevertheless, Alexios managed to recover part of the lost territories in Asia Minor.

John II Komnenos

After Alexios's death in 1118, power was taken over by his son John II Komnenos. He continued the policy of strengthening the state, but without the major internal conflicts that had marked previous years.

John II was known as a capable military commander and a just ruler. During his reign, the empire regained part of its influence in Asia Minor and the Balkans.

Manuel I Komnenos

The most ambitious ruler of the Komnenian dynasty was Manuel I Komnenos, who reigned from 1143 to 1180.

He aspired to restore old Roman power and pursued an active foreign policy toward Hungary, Italy, the Crusader states, and the Turks.

During the time of Manuel I, Constantinople was once again one of the wealthiest and most significant cities in the world. The imperial court was a center of diplomacy, trade, and culture.

Crisis After Manuel I

After the death of Manuel I in 1180, the empire again entered a period of instability. The underage Emperor Alexios II fell under the influence of court factions, which led to political conflicts in the capital.

In 1182, power was taken over by Andronikos I Komnenos. Although he initially tried to limit the power of the aristocracy and implement reforms, his rule became increasingly harsh and provoked great resistance.

The End of the Komnenian Dynasty

Andronikos I was overthrown and killed in 1185, thus ending the rule of the Komnenoi. The Angelos dynasty came to the throne, but the empire was already significantly weakened.

The period from 1056 to 1185 represented a time of great falls and great restorations. Despite territorial losses, the Komnenoi managed to temporarily restore the strength of the Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantium) and preserve its position as a great power.
