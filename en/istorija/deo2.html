518–610

From the accession of Justin I to the rise of Heraclius — the age of Justinian the Great, the greatest territorial expansion, and the beginning of great trials for the empire.

The Period of Restoration of the Roman Empire

In 518, Justin I ascended the throne, an experienced soldier of humble origin. Although he did not have a high education, he laid the foundations for a new era of the empire's rise and secured the succession for his nephew Justinian.

With the accession of Justinian I in 527, one of the most significant reigns in the history of the Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantium) began. His goal was the restoration of the unified Roman Empire, both in political and military terms, as well as in legal and religious aspects.

Justinian's Conquests

Under the command of great generals Belisarius and Narses, the Eastern Roman armies reconquered North Africa from the Vandals, Italy from the Ostrogoths, and part of southern Hispania. The empire reached its greatest territorial extent after the fall of the Western Roman Empire.

Although these conquests represented great success, they required enormous financial resources and heavily burdened the state administration and military.

Corpus Juris Civilis

One of the greatest achievements of Justinian's reign was the enactment of the Corpus Juris Civilis, a collection of Roman law that for centuries represented the basis of the legal system of a large part of Europe.

This codification enabled the preservation of the Roman legal tradition and became the foundation of modern civil law in numerous European states.

Hagia Sophia and the Nika Riot

In 532, Constantinople was engulfed by the great Nika Riot. The population's dissatisfaction turned into an armed uprising that seriously threatened Justinian's rule. Thanks to the decisive intervention of generals Belisarius and Mundus, the riot was suppressed, and the city was subsequently thoroughly rebuilt.

On the site of the old church, the magnificent Hagia Sophia was erected, one of the greatest architectural achievements of the Middle Ages. Its dome became a symbol of Constantinople and the entire Christian East.

Wars with Persia

In addition to conquests in the west, the empire continuously fought against the Sasanian Persia in the east. These prolonged wars consumed enormous human and material resources, but both powers continued the conflict until the beginning of the 7th century.

The End of a Great Era

After Justinian's death in 565, the empire gradually faced increasing pressures. There followed the invasions of the Lombards into Italy, the Avars and Slavs into the Balkans, as well as the continuation of wars with Persia.

By 610, the state had entered a deep crisis. With the overthrow of Emperor Phocas and the accession of Heraclius, a new era of Eastern Roman history began.
