717–867

From Leo III the Isaurian to Basil I — the era of the empire's defense, Iconoclasm, and gradual recovery.

Leo III the Isaurian and the Salvation of the Empire

In 717, Leo III the Isaurian ascended the imperial throne. He inherited a state weakened by long wars with the Arabs and the loss of major provinces. His greatest task was the defense of Constantinople and the preservation of the very existence of the empire.

The greatest challenge came that same year when a major Arab campaign besieged Constantinople. Thanks to the strong walls, good organization of defense, and the use of Greek fire, the Eastern Romans managed to withstand the siege of 717–718.

This victory was one of the most significant in medieval history. Constantinople remained a bulwark that prevented further Arab expansion toward Europe.

Iconoclasm

In addition to external enemies, the empire was shaken by a great internal religious conflict known as Iconoclasm. In 726, Leo III began a policy prohibiting the veneration of holy icons, believing that their worship was contrary to the Christian faith.

This decision caused deep divisions between supporters and opponents of Iconoclasm. The conflict lasted more than a century and strongly influenced the politics, church, and culture of the empire.

Constantine V

The son of Leo III, Constantine V, continued the policy of Iconoclasm. He was a capable military commander who successfully fought against the Arabs and Bulgars, but he remained a very controversial figure due to his harsh treatment of opponents of religious reforms.

During his time, the Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantium) strengthened militarily, but internal religious divisions continued to burden the state.

Restoration of Icons and End of the Conflict

After the death of Constantine V, the empire gradually began to change its attitude toward Iconoclasm. Empress Irene convened the Second Council of Nicaea in 787, at which the veneration of holy icons was restored.

However, the conflict was not completely over. At the beginning of the 9th century, Iconoclasm was temporarily revived, but it was finally defeated in 843 during the reign of Empress Theodora, wife of Emperor Theophilos and regent for their son Michael III. This event is known as the Triumph of Orthodoxy.

The Amorian Dynasty

During the 9th century, the empire entered a period of gradual stabilization. The Amorian dynasty continued the restoration of state institutions and the strengthening of military organization.

Although the Arabs still posed a threat, the Eastern Romans gradually regained strength. The military system of themes became the foundation of the empire's defense, and culture and education began to develop again.

The Beginning of the Macedonian Dynasty

In 867, Basil I the Macedonian came to power, the founder of the Macedonian dynasty. His accession marked the beginning of one of the most glorious periods in the history of the Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantium).

In the coming centuries, the empire would experience great political, military, and cultural prosperity known as the Macedonian Renaissance.
