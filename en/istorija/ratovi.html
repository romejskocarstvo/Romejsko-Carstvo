Great Wars of the Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantium)

More than a thousand years of struggle for the survival of the last heir of Rome.

An Empire in Constant Struggle

The Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantium) was one of the longest-lasting states in world history. During more than eleven centuries of its existence, the empire almost continuously waged wars against various enemies — Persians, Arabs, Bulgars, Normans, Crusaders, and Ottomans.

Unlike many empires that relied solely on military strength, the Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantium) based its survival on a combination of military power, diplomacy, fortifications, economic strength, and the ability to adapt to new conditions.

Constantinople, the largest city of the Christian world, represented the heart of this system. Its walls, fleet, and state administration enabled the empire to survive numerous crises.

Justinian's Wars of Restoration (527–565)

When Justinian I ascended the throne in 527, he set the goal of restoring the former Roman Empire and reclaiming the lost western provinces.

To achieve this goal, he relied on great generals Belisarius and Narses.

War Against the Vandals

In 533, Belisarius launched a campaign against the Vandal Kingdom in North Africa. After victory over king Gelimer, Carthage was reintegrated into the Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantium) in 534.

This conquest represented a great symbolic success because Roman rule was reestablished in Africa.

Gothic Wars in Italy

In 535, a great war was begun against the Ostrogoths in Italy. The conflict lasted more than twenty years and was one of the most destructive wars of late antiquity.

Eastern Roman forces conquered Rome and Ravenna, but the fighting left Italy severely devastated.

Wars with the Sasanian Empire

The Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantium) for centuries waged wars with Sasanian Persia for control of eastern territories, trade routes, and political influence.

These conflicts represented one of the longest-lasting rivalries in history.

The Last Great Roman-Persian War (602–628)

The greatest conflict between the Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantium) and Sasanian Persia broke out at the beginning of the 7th century. The Persian shah Khosrow II exploited the crisis in Constantinople and launched a great campaign.

Persian armies conquered Syria, Palestine, and Egypt. Jerusalem fell in 614, and the relic of the True Cross was taken to Persia.

Emperor Heraclius launched a great counterattack and managed to inflict decisive defeats on the Persians. By the peace of 628, the empire regained the lost territories.

Arab Conquests and the Struggle for Survival

Only a few years after victory over Persia, the Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantium) faced a new danger — the Arab conquests.

During the 7th century, the richest eastern provinces were lost: Syria, Palestine, and Egypt.

Nevertheless, Constantinople remained unconquered thanks to its walls, fleet, and Greek fire.

First Arab Siege of Constantinople (674–678)

The Arab Caliphate attempted to conquer the capital of the Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantium), but failed to breach the city's defenses.

The Eastern Roman fleet, using Greek fire, destroyed enemy ships and forced the Arabs to withdraw.

The victory of Constantinople represented one of the most important moments in the history of Europe.

Second Arab Siege of Constantinople (717–718)

The greatest attempt at Arab conquest of Constantinople occurred in 717 during the reign of emperor Leo III the Isaurian.

A huge Arab army surrounded the city from land and sea, but the Theodosian Walls and Eastern Roman fleet proved invincible.

Greek fire, lack of food, and harsh winter forced the Arabs to withdraw in 718.

Wars with the Bulgars

After the Arab danger, the Bulgars became one of the greatest adversaries of the Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantium) in the Balkans.

Relations between the two states alternated between wars, alliances, and diplomatic agreements.

Krum and the Great Bulgar Danger

At the beginning of the 9th century, the Bulgar khan Krum became one of the most dangerous enemies of the empire.

In 811, emperor Nikephoros I fell in a campaign against the Bulgars, which was one of the most severe Eastern Roman defeats of the Middle Ages.

Krum planned an attack on Constantinople, but died in 814 before the campaign.

Simeon the Great and the Struggle for the Imperial Title

In the 10th century, the Bulgar tsar Simeon the Great created a powerful state and attempted to succeed the Eastern Roman imperial authority.

After his death in 927, Bulgar power began to decline, which allowed the Eastern Romans to regain the initiative.

Basil II and the Fall of the First Bulgarian Empire

Emperor Basil II waged long wars against Bulgaria. After decades of fighting, the Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantium) in 1018 destroyed the First Bulgarian Empire.

This victory represented the peak of military power of the Macedonian dynasty.

Normans and the Loss of Southern Italy

After great victories of the 10th and 11th centuries, the Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantium) faced a new enemy from the west — the Normans.

Norman warriors gradually created powerful states in southern Italy and began to conquer the last Eastern Roman territories.

During the 11th century, the empire lost almost all of its possessions in Italy. Robert Guiscard managed to conquer the last Eastern Roman territories on the southern part of the peninsula.

The loss of Italy was a great blow because those territories for centuries had been part of the Roman heritage.

Crusades and New Enemies

At the end of the 11th century, the Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantium) found itself between Islamic states to the east and Western European Crusaders who passed through its lands.

Emperor Alexios I Komnenos requested aid from the West against the Seljuks, which led to the First Crusade in 1096.

Although initially allies, relations between the Eastern Romans and Crusaders over time became increasingly complex.

Fourth Crusade and the Fall of Constantinople 1204

The heaviest blow that the Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantium) suffered from Western forces occurred during the Fourth Crusade.

In 1204, the Crusaders conquered Constantinople, plundered the city, and established the Latin Empire.

Although the empire was restored in 1261 under the Palaiologoi, it never again reached its former strength.

The Final Struggle Against the Ottomans

After the restoration of Constantinople in 1261, the Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantium) existed for almost two more centuries, but gradually lost territories.

The Ottomans expanded from Asia Minor toward the Balkans, while Eastern Roman emperors attempted to gain aid from Western states.

By the 15th century, the empire controlled mainly Constantinople and small surrounding areas.

Battle of Nicopolis (1396)

A Crusader army assembled in 1396 attempted to halt Ottoman expansion, but sultan Bayezid I won a great victory.

The defeat at Nicopolis showed the strength of the Ottoman Empire and further worsened the position of Constantinople.

The Last Defense of Constantinople

In the mid-15th century, emperor Constantine XI Palaiologos inherited a state that was only a remnant of the former empire.

Sultan Mehmed II prepared a great campaign against Constantinople with a huge army and powerful artillery.

The siege began on April 6, 1453. Despite the small number of defenders, the city resisted attacks for more than fifty days.

The Fall of Constantinople and the End of the Empire

On May 29, 1453, the Ottomans breached the defenses of Constantinople. Emperor Constantine XI Palaiologos fell defending the last remnants of the Roman Empire.

With the fall of the city, the Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantium) ceased to exist, a state that for more than a thousand years had preserved the Roman tradition in the East.

Its legacy continued to live through law, culture, art, and Christian tradition.

Conclusion — An Empire That Survived Centuries

The great wars of the Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantium) show the history of a state that survived for centuries thanks to its army, diplomacy, and ability to adapt.

From Justinian's conquests to the last defense of Constantinople, the Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantium) remained one of the most significant heirs of ancient Rome.
