1261–1453

The last centuries of the Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantium) — the age of the Palaiologoi, the struggle for survival, and the last defenses of Constantinople.

The Palaiologos Dynasty

After the restoration of Constantinople in 1261, Michael VIII Palaiologos ascended the throne, the founder of the last Eastern Roman imperial dynasty. His greatest achievement was the return of the capital, but the restored state was considerably weaker than before.

Michael VIII attempted to preserve the empire through diplomacy, forming alliances with Western powers and preventing new Crusader campaigns against Constantinople.

Consequences of the Fourth Crusade

Although the capital was returned, the consequences of the conquest of 1204 were never fully overcome. The empire lost a large part of its economic strength, fleet, and numerous territories.

During the 14th century, the Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantium) gradually shrank. Balkan territories were lost, and a new power from the east — the Ottoman Empire — became an ever greater threat.

Andronikos II and the Weakening of the State

During the reign of Andronikos II Palaiologos, the empire continued to weaken. The army was reduced, and dependence on foreign mercenaries became an increasingly serious problem.

At the same time, the Ottomans began to conquer Byzantine territories in Asia Minor, gradually pushing the Eastern Romans out of their ancestral homeland.

The Rise of the Ottomans

At the beginning of the 14th century, the Ottomans created a powerful state that gradually expanded its rule in the Balkans. The Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantium) became a shadow of its former self, with limited territories around Constantinople.

The Fall of Constantinople in 1453

The siege of Constantinople began on April 6, 1453. The Ottoman army of Sultan Mehmed II was numerically superior and possessed powerful artillery, while the defenders of the city were few in number.

Despite the enormous difference in strength, the Eastern Romans successfully defended the city walls for more than fifty days. Volunteers from Western Europe fought alongside them, among whom Giovanni Giustiniani particularly distinguished himself.

On May 29, 1453, the Ottomans breached the defenses. Emperor Constantine XI Palaiologos fell on the ramparts, defending the last remnants of the Roman Empire.

The End of the Eastern Roman Empire

With the fall of Constantinople, the Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantium) ceased to exist, a state that for more than eleven centuries had preserved the Roman state tradition in the East.

Nevertheless, its legacy did not disappear. Eastern Roman diplomacy, law, art, literature, and Christian tradition continued to influence the development of Europe and the world.

The Legacy of the Eastern Romans

The Eastern Roman Empire (Byzantium) left one of the most significant legacies in human history. It preserved a large part of ancient knowledge, developed a unique culture, and represented a bridge between ancient Rome and medieval Europe.

From Constantine the Great to Constantine XI Palaiologos, the history of the Eastern Romans represents a story of perseverance, adaptation, and the preservation of one of the longest-lasting civilizations in history.
